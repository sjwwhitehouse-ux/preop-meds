Blood thinners

There are many and varied reasons to be taking blood thinners, including for management of blood clots, atrial fibrillation or vascular disease, or after a stroke or heart attack.

Do you take any blood thinners or anti-clot medicines?
Please select an option.
⚠ Important guidance
Many blood thinners need a specific stop date based on your surgery, kidney function, and whether a spinal or epidural is planned. It is usually the responsibility of the surgeon to advise on management of your blood thinners, however sometimes your anaesthetist may give specific advice.

Do not stop these medicines unless your surgical or anaesthetic team tells you to.

GLP-1 Agonists

This group of medications is used primarily for diabetes and weight loss, but there are other emerging indications.

This class includes
  • Semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy)
  • Dulaglutide (Trulicity)
  • Liraglutide (Saxenda)
  • Tirzepatide (Mounjaro)
Do you take any of these drugs?
Please select an option.
⚠ Aspiration risk — please read carefully
These medications can slow down the transit of food from the stomach to the small bowel. Under anaesthesia this significantly increases the risk of aspiration (where stomach contents can obstruct or contaminate the lungs), a potentially life-threatening complication. Because this risk is not reduced by simply ceasing the drug, it is generally advised that you continue this medication.

To manage this risk, you must undertake an extended fast prior to surgery. From 24 hours before your admission, you must omit all food from your diet and may only drink clear fluids*. For the final 6 hours before admission you must not take anything by mouth, even water. Failure to comply may lead to your procedure being cancelled.

* Clear fluids are fully transparent and contain no particles. They include:
  • Water and ice chips
  • Pulpless, sediment-free juice (e.g. apple)
  • Cordial
  • Black tea or coffee (NO milk or cream)
  • Sports drinks and clear sodas
  • Clear, fat-free broths
  • Plain jellies (no fruit or toppings)

SGLT-2 Inhibitors

You may be on these drugs for management of diabetes, or for heart or kidney health.

This class includes the following as single or combined products
  • Dapagliflozin (Forxiga, Xigduo)
  • Empagliflozin (Jardiance, Jardiamet, Glyxambi)
  • Ertugliflozin (Steglatro, Segluromet, Steglujan)
  • Canagliflozin (Invokana)
Do you take any of the above?
Please select an option.
⚠ Euglycaemic ketoacidosis risk
Certain stresses (including prolonged fasting, bowel prep and surgery itself) can cause 'euglycaemic ketoacidosis' — a potentially life-threatening complication — in patients taking these medications, especially those with diabetes. This risk reduces when the drug is withheld prior to surgery.

For minor (day case) surgery, you must omit this medication on the day of surgery.

For all other surgery requiring overnight admission, and for patients undergoing bowel preparation prior to colonoscopy, you must omit this medication on the day of surgery as well as the 2 days prior.

If unsure, follow the instructions for overnight admission (cease day of surgery and for two days prior).

During your admission your blood sugar levels and ketone levels will be monitored closely, and you will be encouraged to eat and drink as soon as possible after surgery.

Insulins

For management of type I and II diabetes.

Do you use insulins?
Please select an option.
Insulin instructions
Insulin should not be administered unless you are eating regularly and normally. Once you commence your preoperative fast, you should not take any insulin. Monitor your blood sugar levels closely and seek medical advice in the event of hypo- or hyperglycaemic events, or if feeling unwell.

If you are booked for SURGERY IN THE MORNING, you should:
  • Eat a normal dinner the night before
  • Take half your long-acting insulin dose
  • Take your usual short-acting dose
Omit all insulins on the morning of surgery.

If you are booked for SURGERY IN THE AFTERNOON you should:
  • Eat an early breakfast before commencing your fast
  • Take half your long-acting insulin dose
  • Take your usual short-acting dose at breakfast time
Omit any further insulin while fasting.

If you do not eat breakfast, you should omit all insulin and monitor your blood sugar levels closely.

If you use an insulin pump, you should cease the infusion during your fast but may keep it attached and monitor your blood sugar levels closely.

Blood pressure medications

Steroids
In some circumstances you may require extra steroids on the day of surgery and/or the following days to help your body deal with the physiologic stress of surgery.
Do you take naltrexone? (This may be prescribed for management of alcohol and drug disorders, or to manage autoimmune or chronic pain conditions.)
Please select an option.
⚠ Naltrexone (including low-dose naltrexone)
It is important to cease this medication for 72 hours prior to surgery as it will otherwise reduce the effect of the strong pain killers you may require during and after your operation. You can recommence this drug once you are no longer requiring strong pain killers to manage your postoperative pain.
Do you take any 'older-style' antidepressants such as phenelzine (Nardil), tranylcypromine (Parnate) or moclobemide (Aurorix)?
Please select an option.
⚠ MAOI antidepressants — contact your anaesthetist
These drugs can have serious life-threatening interactions with other drugs used during anaesthesia. Your anaesthetist will provide a personalised plan to cease these preoperatively. That may require input from your psychiatrist to ensure steady management of your underlying psychiatric condition.
